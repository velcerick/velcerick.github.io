History

History

Al Pa'anch Shahir is an old name, one passed along based on snippets of ancient text and old maps, describing the region surrounding the Five Cities.  It is a name carried on ancient maps and in ancient texts rescued from the Great Library in the accursed old Al Harahi.  One such text, the only of its kind, describes the events leading up to the formation of the Five Cities and the raising of the Wall.  Scholars argue over the validity of the text, but as it is the only text known to describe these events, it is all they have to go by.  An excerpt of the text, entitled Shai Rodon: A History, is below.

The text describes events leading up to the Sundering and occur circa 10990.

Chaos

Opening our eyes to the dangers that were to come was a gradual process.  First came a band of Nyss, elves from the high peaks almost never seen in the region.  They made their way from village to village, city to city, spreading word that a great evil had destroyed their civilization, and to ready ourselves.  We should have recognized that the terror in the story of the Nyss warriors was something we should fear, instead we took their tales of a shaggy beast a thousand feet tall for exaggeration, of endless storms and gathering evil across the mountains as far and isolated from us.  When a bank of dark clouds rolled in from the east some weeks later, blanketing the region in storm, we did not make the connection, not until the morning.

In the morning, chaos had descended upon the land.  Madness struck the region like a plague.  The streets of Shai Rodon  ran red with blood, the air filled with the howls of the grieving and tortured, mixed with the laughter of the insane.  The next several months were a dark period for the region, marked by outbreaks of madness and murder, endless nights of horrific nightmares.  Just as the madness was getting under control, the attacks began.  Sahuagin and kuo-toa emerged from the depths in waves, killing and kidnapping.  From the mountains giants began to descend, madness in their eyes.  Gnolls came in from the desert, and creatures unknown from the dark heart of the jungles.  Somewhere far to the east, in the mountains, a low rumble could be heard always, and at times there were changes in the distant horizon as if the mountains themselves were moving.  We called a council of rulers at Shai Rodon to make a plan.

Long deliberation culminated in a decision to send out emissaries to all the towns and villages in the region to rally at Shai Rodon.  Local leaders and nobility would maintain their title, and city leaders would be given a seat at council.  Sacrifices of our own sovereignty were necessary for the plan to have any hope of success.  However, there was still the problem of who to send.  It was discovered early on that bands of soldiers or even adventurers quickly broke down on the road, succumbing to the oppression of the storms and eventually descending into madness, and individuals could not be relied on for their sanity either.  A suggestion was made that we call upon the aid of the necromancers whose undead servitors were immune to the maddening plague.  The decision was not made lightly, necromancy is a dark and evil art, and we were unsure whether they stood with us in this fight, but they answered our pleas, and soon a hundred undead, disguised to appear as living mortals were making their way in all directions to give the call.

Gathering

The first of the refugees began arriving almost immediately, mostly humans and halflings from nearby villages and settlements who would have likely come to some city or another eventually anyway.   There was a lull then, and for several weeks the refugees slowed to a trickle.  Just as we began to count the effort a failure, a great host arrived from the east.  Apparently an intrepid band of adventurers had worked to help convince the nobility of a now forgotten city that they should answer the call, and as the city emptied its walls and marched toward Shai Rodon they gathered up all the smaller villages along the march.  That first arrival was like the opening of a floodgate, as from east and west and north similar bands, none fewer than a thousand in number began to pour into rapidly growing city.  Day after day they came, and while it seemed certain all the cities and towns in the region had congregated, an even greater surprise came.

As the tide of newcomers was once again beginning to slow, the dragonborn appeared from the west.  From the shadow of the storm they moved into view, like a sea of scintillating colors, marching toward Shai Rodon.  Great drakes moved among them carrying building-sized wagons laden with heavy burdens or great siege engines.  We scarcely had a chance to think on how to respond to this new threat when word came that a chasm had suddenly opened to the north, and creatures were pouring forth from its gaping maw like termites from a kicked mound.

It was not long after the mysterious appearance of the chasm that dragonborn fliers arrived.  They requested audience with our leaders, which was given freely.  We assumed it would be time to negotiate the terms of our surrender.  It was not so, however, instead they brought word that their lands were under attack in much the same way as the lands of Al Pa’anch Shahir, and that they were responding to a call to arms by a pale messenger.   Never had we considered that the necromancer’s emissaries would continue into the lands of the dragonborn, and even if we had we would have never expected a response.  As long as history is recorded the dragonborn have lived to the west, a noble, proud, and militant race with had little care for the goings on of other races.  Yet, here they were, like heroes out of a tale, to fight at our side against the growing darkness.

Just as the news was sinking in, that the city was about to increase in size by nearly half again its current overloaded population, word came down from the northern guard that the host moving in from the chasm was similarly not hostile.  Rather, it was led by mountain dwarves carrying banners of peace.  Even more so than with the dragonborn, there would have never been any expectation of an answer from the mountain dwarves, who we assumed would be secure in their cavernous hold.  Yet like the dragonborn, they saw the wisdom in the rally, and they came to fight, their powerful elementalist shamans having carved a tunnel deep through the earth all the way from the far northern mountains, to open near Shai Rodon.  With the arrival of the dwarves and dragonborn the population of Shai Rodon doubled almost instantaneously.  The dwarves brought with them a smaller band of other beings from the depths - drow, shadar-kai, kobolds, and even stranger visitors.   Similarly, over the next few days, as the Dragonborn began to arrive, here and there handfuls of tribal creatures from above ground, a small band of goliaths from the mountains here, a pack of goblins there, arrived in the city, all carrying word that they had come in response to an invitation by one of the undead emissaries.

Shai Rodon now had easily ten times what had once been considered a respectably large population.  Its walls were filled to bursting, and the refugee encampments outside the walls stretched for miles in every direction.  Despite the logistical challenges of managing so great a number, we were held together by the constant struggle against the closing darkness.  It was immediately clear that the plan was a success.   The population now included thousands of magic users and tens of thousands, perhaps hundreds of thousands, of capable warriors to hold back any assault on the population.  Legions of hedge-wizards and priests worked to keep the madness at bay.  A sort of uneasy stability seemed to settle in. 

The Wall

Some time after the arrival of the dragonborn and the dwarves, the rumbling in the east culminated in a great crash and eruption.  On the distant horizon, where the mountains loomed gray and jagged a reddish glow could be seen, a speck amidst a sea of ruddy brown.  Although magical scrying had long since become impossible and dangerous, leading to visions which drove their casters mad, more mundane means were used to spy the distant fire somewhat more closely.  From the city’s observatory came word that an entire mountain had erupted, sending a plume of flame and magma miles into the air.  However, it was the cause of the eruption that caused them to react with haste.  They claimed that a snaking line of black could be seen coiled about a nearby mountain and running down into the valley.  It appeared to be a black river of unknown origin or length.

Within a few days the “river” could occasionally be seen among the mountains with the naked eye.  It would slither up or down the mountains like a hair-thin black line among the usually uniform mountain orange and disappear later the same day or several days past.  As it moved toward closer mountains it grew thicker, and the observatory eventually reported after several observations that it was perhaps one hundred miles long and a half mile wide, and that it was more likely some serpent or worm of unheard of size, as it seemed to cast a shadow beneath it.   Further, as the Serpent moved nearer, the scattered attacks on the city became larger and more focused, bringing with them wave after wave of madness and death.

We called a great council of leaders, both leaders of nobility and leaders of the various arcane, divine, and militant societies.  Representatives were present from the elite warrior societies of the dragonborn, from the dark societies of the necromancers, from the Shan Del, and more, but an agreement on a plan was not quick in arriving.  It was the dwarven Stonewardens, a spiritual sect of mountain dwarves who used large pieces of bonded earth elarius to shape stone and call forth elementals who finally spoke up with a plan.  They told a most extraordinary story, indicating that in their hold, a stony fortress drawn up from the red bedrock beneath the earth, they had created a vortex to Essence, specifically, to the region of Essence between the crushing press of Gaia and the scintillating radiance of the Glittering Havens.  Here they found a slumbering hero, a god among the mountain dwarves, Grombuur Mountainroot, Lord of the Earth and Mineral, the First Mountain  Dwarf.  While it may be dangerous to waken the slumbering dwarf, they felt certain he would help.

So it was that a singularly extraordinary dwarf emerged from the hold of the Stonewardens to meet the waiting council of Shai Rodon.  He was sized much like a normal dwarf, squat and sturdy, though perhaps somewhat thicker and broader than average.  Upon his head rested a singularly odd and mundane looking helm, a great pitted and dented thing of steel with exaggerated wings forged for a head obviously larger than his own.  From beneath the helm a great mass of course brown hair draped to his feet stiff and sparkling as if encrusted in salt or gemstone.  A knobby nose protruded from the mass, his deep set, dark eyes heavily shadowed by great bushy eyebrows.  His arms and legs and feet were covered in thick plates of armor of some unknown metal, though his hands were bare and empty, his skin dark and ruddy.   A great, two bladed axe of plain iron poked through the hair hanging down his back.  Though imposing, he did not look a god.

He appraised us, the waiting council, as we appraised him.  It was clear he could see the doubt on our faces, and he suddenly raised his harms, palms upraised, and the earth awoke.  All around the encampment, in a ring no less than fifty miles in diameter, a great stone wall rose.  Made of the same deep red bedrock of the mountains it lurched forth from some untold depth, rising and rising until it towered easily five hundred feet into the air, its top tapered to a keen edge like a blade.  Grombuur then clenched his upraised hands into fists, and the rising of the massive wall shuddered to a stop, as it did so much of the rock crumbled away to dust, leaving behind not just a wall, but more a great fortress.  Stairs were carved into the stone, and at regular intervals great ledges spanned, open from the inside and running all along the wall’s length.   He pointed in the direction of the mouth of the river and toward the sea, and a great grating sound was heard, followed by a distant rush.  We saw then that we were quite mistaken, and we stared at him, many dumbfounded by such a feat of magic as none of us had ever seen.  He gave a satisfied sounding grunt, and walked back into the elementalists’ hold without speaking a word.

The wall made Shai Rodon as defensible as a mountaintop fortress against the attacks.  It was soon discovered that there were massive portcullises of some lightweight, but intensely strong metal across great gates where the rivers crossed Grombuur’s Wall.  All along the length of the wall battlements had been set, easily accessible from the interior, but extremely inaccessible from the exterior.  In its 100 feet of thickness it also hid rooms and caverns and chambers filled with weapons and armor which appeared forged by master craftsmen, enough to suit and arm ten thousand soldiers.  There was only one problem; it was too big.  The wall enclosed an area fifty miles across, large portions of which were nearly impassible jungle.  So it was that the council decided to break the populace up into five distinct groups, each made up of an admixture of races and citizens from various regions so as not to encourage future warring, and each situated along the great rivers within the wall.
          

The Serpent

For a time, despite the continuous storms, ever present madness and nightmare, and the constant battles along the wall against increasingly bizarre and powerful creatures, things were well for the people of Shai Rodon.  Somehow, with the great wall blocking the shifting view of the distant horizon, and with our battle against the invading hordes now seemingly a foregone victory, we let ourselves forget about the strange black shape that had snaked about in the distance.  We forgot about it until it emerged suddenly from the eastern bay.  Though we had known of its immense size, the knowing was nothing compared to actually seeing it.  It loomed great and massive, towering thousands of feet above our great wall.  Its massive body was wider than the rivers, and grotesque to behold.  Rather than a single black serpent, it was a writhing mass of dark tendrils, thick and thin.  Many were covered in wicked barbs, or had long jagged slits where hungry maws gaped, bloated orifices spouted dark, putrid ichor into the bay.  Worse still, from within the folds and creases of its slimy form thousands of creatures were constantly birthed.  There were bloated winged things, all hooks and tentacles, swimming creatures, creatures covered in suckers and hair, swimming and crawling, flying and slithering, creatures of all types and of all sizes, all from the darkest and unknown pits of nightmare.

Like rotted fruit they burst as arrows and magic and siege weaponry tore into them.  They were immolated in flame and smashed by hurtling stones called by the elementalists.  But there were too many.  More quickly than they could be defeated the great Serpent gave birth to more.  It spewed forth a great stream of putrescent yellowish fluid which slapped against the eastern wall, draining down toward the portcullis at the mouth of the river.  Acrid black smoke rose as acid ate away at the wall.  Druids called upon the spirits of the river to cleanse the acid yet it clung to the stone as if it were a part of it, pitting and weakening the great wall.  In a sudden swift move the Serpent darted forth covering a distance of many miles in the time it takes a snake to strike.  It slammed into the weakened wall with a sickening sound of rent flesh and tearing stone.

The Serpent let out a high pitched and hideous scream as hundreds of feet of its mass erupted in a shower of putrid ichor.  Thousands of creatures burst forth with it, many of them little more than half formed masses of organic gore.  At the same time, the eroded section wall burst inward, and the great portcullis, with no walls to hold it up, toppled.  As the serpent rose again, a great stone disk whirred through the air and sliced deeply into its side.   It reacted immediately, arcing sharply up over the wall and darting downward toward the source of the attack, an insignificant speck of a dwarf standing atop a mound of red stone near the city of Shai Rodon.   It covered the distance, more than fifty miles, swiftly.  It would be on the dwarf in seconds.

Grombuur stood atop the tower, waiting.  As the beast rushed toward him, its tentacled body unraveling at the end to form a great and grasping mass of feelers rimmed with fangs and teeth, dripping with foul slime, he waited.  Then, just seconds from impact, he drew back his axe over his head and swung.  It was as if the air crystallized along the arc of the swing and the creature had fallen upon a razors edge large enough to rend it in two.   The Serpent, in all of its intense momentum, parted like a flow of water around the knife of crystallized air, spilling a torrent of acid down upon the tower and the lonely dwarf.  For more than half of its length the creature was split, and its massive bulk came crashing down across both the western and eastern edges of the great wall, the wall’s razor edge slicing its length neatly into three pieces.  As it died, thousands of creatures struggled to escape from its body, hoping to find birth before their life giving progenitor passed.

Slowly it seemed, the Serpent died, its great mass lashing out black tentacles that sizzled and melted into a trench of acid all around its body, burrowing a great chasm through the earth.  The sinking mass carved a great channel through the land, joining the eastern bay with the southern coast, breaking the wall in two places and separating one of the five settlements from the rest of Shai Rodon.  Perhaps the acid still eats away at the ocean floor, and one day the sea will drain in to the depths of the Underdark.  Of Grombuur or the elementalists there was no sign.

After the Serpent died, the people of Shai Rodon were able to fight off its surviving brood.  Over the next several months, the battles continued to rage.  They were hard fought and bloody, though none claimed so many lives as the fight against the Serpent, and none had such grave consequences.  Thousands were lost before the coming of the Sundering.

The Light

The following passage is assumed to be from Shai Rodon: A History as it was found among loose pages from the same expedition as the discovery of the original text.  It is unclear how much time elapsed between the successful defeat of the Serpent and the events described.  This event is a description of the end of the Sundering and occurred in 10992. 

It was at a particularly dark hour for Shai Rodon that the Light came upon us.   The massing armies of the nightmare host pressed in all around the Wall.  Mixed in among the enemies of old, giants and gnolls and orcs, were hordes of hideous formless and faceless things which gibbered and shrieked, whose voices could be heard at all hours of day or night.  Though the Wall held, the rivers carried upon them disease and sickness which corrupted all who drank from them.  People and animals grew mad or contracted wasting diseases; some transformed into tentacled beasts, or melted into horrific living oozes.  Even the plants grew bloated and foul, some gaining ambulation, others producing spores which carried madness on the wind, others still giving birth to even more horrific creatures.  From the skies above faceless winged things struck out in the dark of night, kidnapping children and spreading madness wherever they were spied.  Worse still, legions of deformed fish-like halfmen constantly moved in from the acidic trench left behind when the Serpent fell, and our southern settlement, cut off from the rest of Shai Rodon, was on the brink of being wholly overrun.  Then the Light came.

It was first spied by a lookout in the northernmost tower of the Wall.  A shimmering curtain of light was growing in the north, and moving fast toward us.  Not long after he called down the word, the entire horizon to the north was a white wall of light as bright as staring into the noonday sun.  As the brilliance approached, a sort of silence fell over the land.  Though it lasted only a moment, it is something that will be remembered by all who witnessed it.  The Light brought with it a feeling of unparalleled warmth and peace, goodness and rightness, a sharp contrast to the horrifying wrongness that had suffused our land for so long.  When it passed, all was changed in an instant.  For the first time in close to a year, a clear sky shone above Shai Rodon.  As we looked down upon the battlefield, or out into the sea, we marveled at what we saw.  The nightmare armies had vanished.  In the blink of an eye they were gone, only a remnant of rabble remained, creatures confused at the sudden disappearance of the bulk of their forces, creatures who feared the bright light of the sun shining down.  They broke and fled, and many were slaughtered as they ran.  Shai Rodon was free.
